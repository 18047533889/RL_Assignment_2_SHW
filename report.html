Model architecture — why this shape

  • Residual CNN + SE (192 channels, 8 blocks): The board needs hierarchical spatial reasoning (sub-boards, cross-level column wins). Stacking eight residual blocks with squeeze-and-excitation increases receptive field and lets channels reweight after each stage without exploding parameters.
  • Value head MLP $144\to 768\to 1$: A wider critic helps GAE targets in long, stochastic episodes; policy head stays lighter (1×1 conv to 2 channels + linear to 96 logits).
  • ~5.7M params: Chosen as a capacity vs training time compromise: beyond this size, iter_seconds grows quickly (see iter_seconds.png).

    1. Problem Formulation & Game Rules

    This assignment implements a Super Tic-Tac-Toe variant played on a triangular board. Two players alternate placing noughts (O) and crosses (X). The game is modelled as a two-player zero-sum stochastic game.

    Formal Game Definition

    We model the problem as a Markov Game (stochastic game) $\mathcal{G} = (\mathcal{N}, \mathcal{S}, \{\mathcal{A}_i\}, \mathcal{T}, \{R_i\}, \gamma)$:

    ElementDefinitionConcrete value
    $\mathcal{N}$Player set$\{\text{player\_0 (X)},\; \text{player\_1 (O)}\}$; alternating turns
    $\mathcal{S}$State space12×12 int8 board (0/1/2) + whose turn; $\leq 3^{96}$ reachable states
    $\mathcal{A}_i$Action spaceDiscrete(96) — compact index over 96 valid cells; compact_to_rc/rc_to_compact bijection; occupied cells further masked at runtime
    $\mathcal{T}$TransitionStochastic: chosen cell lands with $P=\frac{1}{2}$; else uniform random neighbour (each $\frac{1}{16}$); invalid/occupied neighbour → forfeit (no placement, turn passes)
    $R_i$RewardZero-sum at every step: $R_{\text{player\_0}} + R_{\text{player\_1}} = 0$. Terminal: win $= +1$, loss $= -1$. Step: placement $= \pm 0.01$, block threat $= +0.10$, create threat $= +0.06$, forfeit $= \mp 0.015$
    $\gamma$Discount0.99

    Rules Summary

    • Board: 6 sub-boards of 4×4, arranged in 3 levels (inverted triangle). Total 96 playable cells on a 12×12 grid (48 cells are padding).
    • Win conditions (first player to achieve any one wins immediately):
      • 4 in a row (horizontal): 4 consecutive same-colour pieces along a row.
      • 4 in a column (vertical): 4 consecutive same-colour pieces along a column, and at least one piece must be in a different level from the others. 4 in a single level does not count.
      • 5 on a diagonal: 5 consecutive same-colour pieces on a $(+1,+1)$ or $(+1,-1)$ diagonal.
    • Stochastic placement: Only $\frac{1}{2}$ probability of landing on the chosen cell. With $\frac{1}{2}$ probability the system picks uniformly among 8 Moore neighbours (each $\frac{1}{16}$). If the neighbour is out-of-bounds, outside the triangle, or occupied → move forfeited (no piece placed, turn passes to opponent).
    • Draw: If all 96 valid cells are occupied and no player has won, the game is a draw (reward 0 for both).
    • Truncation: As a safety valve, episodes are truncated after MAX_STEPS = 200 environment steps (both players combined) with a draw outcome. This prevents degenerate infinite loops in case of logic errors.
    • Turn order: Strict alternation via PettingZoo AEC (Agent-Environment Cycle): player_0 moves first, then player_1, and so on. Terminated agents must call step(None) to advance the turn pointer (PettingZoo protocol).

    Coordinate Mapping

    The RL action space is Discrete(96) — a compact index over the 96 valid triangle cells. The bijection compact_to_rc / rc_to_compact maps compact indices $\{0, \ldots, 95\}$ to valid $(r, c)$ coordinates and back:

    $\text{compact}(r, c) = \texttt{rc\_to\_compact}[r, c] \in \{0, \ldots, 95\} \qquad \text{inverse: } (r, c) = \texttt{compact\_to\_rc}[i]$

    The 48 padding cells in the $12 \times 12$ grid have no compact index — they are excluded from the action space entirely. The action mask (§7) is a 96-dim binary vector that zeros out occupied cells among the 96 valid positions. This compact representation eliminates the need to mask permanently invalid cells and reduces policy head output from 144 to 96 logits.

    Level Assignment

    The function get_level(r) assigns each row to a pyramid level, needed for the cross-level column win rule (§4):

    $\text{level}(r) = \begin{cases} 1 & 0 \leq r < 4 \\[4pt] 2 & 4 \leq r < 8 \\[4pt] 3 & 8 \leq r < 12 \end{cases}$

    Level boundaries occur at rows 3↔4 and 7↔8. crosses_level_boundary(rows) checks len({get_level(r) for r in rows}) > 1.

    3. Stochastic Transition Model

    🧮 Mathematical Insight: MDP Formulation & Probability Distributions

    This section explicitly models the environment as a Markov Decision Process (MDP) with stochastic transitions. By mathematically defining the expected value of actions and mapping the discrete 50% deflection probabilities to spatial coordinates, we provide the RL agent with a rigorous statistical foundation. The forfeit penalty formulation transforms a discontinuous rule (losing the game instantly) into a smooth expected-risk calculation.

    When a player selects an empty cell $(r, c)$, the environment applies the following stochastic process:

    $P(\text{land at }(r,c)) = \frac{1}{2}$

    $P(\text{land at neighbour }(r+\Delta r, c+\Delta c)) = \frac{1}{16} \quad \forall (\Delta r, \Delta c) \in \{-1,0,1\}^2 \setminus \{(0,0)\}$

    If the randomly selected neighbour is:

    • Outside the valid triangle (48 padded cells or out of grid) → move forfeited
    • Already occupied → move forfeited
    • Valid and empty → piece placed there

    Corner Forfeit Analysis

    For a corner cell like $(0, 4)$ (top-left of Level 1):

    NeighbourValid?Reason
    (-1, 3)NoOut of grid
    (-1, 4)NoOut of grid
    (-1, 5)NoOut of grid
    (0, 3)NoOutside triangle
    (0, 5)Yes—
    (1, 3)NoOutside triangle
    (1, 4)Yes—
    (1, 5)Yes—
    $P(\text{forfeit} \mid \text{not direct}) = \frac{5}{8} \quad \Rightarrow \quad P(\text{forfeit total}) = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{5}{8} = \frac{5}{16} \approx 31.25\%$
    Verified by Monte Carlo test (test_stochastic.py::TestMonteCarloConvergence::test_corner_forfeit_rate_mc): Over 20,000 samples, the observed forfeit rate converges to $5/16 \pm 0.02$.

    The forfeit probability depends on how many of the 8 neighbours are invalid or occupied. On an empty board, the forfeit rate varies by position type:

    This means corner and edge positions are inherently riskier — the agent must learn to balance the strategic value of these cells against the higher probability of wasting a turn. Interior cells near the centre of Level 3 have the lowest forfeit risk.

    4. Win Condition Analysis

    4.1 Row Win (4 consecutive horizontal)

    For each row $r$, scan across valid columns for 4 consecutive cells occupied by the same player. Level 1 rows have 4 valid columns, Level 2 rows have 8, and Level 3 rows have 12.

    4.2 Column Win (4 consecutive vertical, cross-level required)

    For each column $c$, scan down valid rows for 4 consecutive same-player cells. Critical constraint: the 4 cells must span at least 2 different levels.

    Key edge case: 4 in a column within a single level (e.g., rows 8,9,10,11 in column 0, all Level 3) does NOT count as a win. This is explicitly tested in test_rules.py::TestColWin::test_4_in_col_same_level_INVALID.

    Level boundaries occur at rows 3↔4 (Level 1↔2) and rows 7↔8 (Level 2↔3). A valid column win must have cells on both sides of at least one boundary.

    4.3 Diagonal Win (5 consecutive)

    Both diagonal directions $(\Delta r, \Delta c) \in \{(1,1), (1,-1)\}$ are checked. Requires 5 consecutive valid cells with the same player's piece. Since each level is only 4 rows tall, a diagonal win of length 5 necessarily spans at least 2 levels (no extra cross-level check needed for diagonals).

    4.4 Draw Detection

    A draw occurs when all 96 valid cells are occupied and no player has won. Implemented as a vectorised check:

    def is_draw(board: np.ndarray) -> bool:
    return not np.any(VALID_MASK & (board == 0))

    Win is checked before draw in env.step(), so a placement that simultaneously fills the board and achieves a win is correctly classified as a win.

Unlike simple MLPs, the residual blocks prevent vanishing gradients in deep layers, while the SE attention mechanism explicitly models cross-channel interdependencies (e.g., correlating the opponent's last move in Channel 3 with the threat heatmap in Channel 4).     5.2 Squeeze-and-Excitation (SE) Block Details

    Each residual block contains an SE channel-attention sub-module that adaptively reweights feature channels. The forward pass of one SE block:

    $\mathbf{z} = \text{GAP}(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{H \times W} \sum_{h,w} \mathbf{x}_{:,h,w} \in \mathbb{R}^C$

    $\mathbf{s} = \sigma\!\big(W_2 \cdot \text{ReLU}(W_1 \cdot \mathbf{z})\big) \in \mathbb{R}^C$

    $\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{c,h,w} = s_c \cdot \mathbf{x}_{c,h,w}$

    where $C = 192$, reduction ratio $r = 4$, bottleneck dimension $= \max(C/r, 1) = 48$. So $W_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{48 \times 192}$, $W_2 \in \mathbb{R}^{192 \times 48}$. Each SE block adds $192 \times 48 + 48 + 48 \times 192 + 192 = 18{,}672$ parameters.

    5.3 Receptive Field Analysis

    Each residual block contains two $3 \times 3$ convolutions (with padding=1, stride=1), expanding the theoretical receptive field by 4 pixels per block:

    After layerTheoretical receptive field
    Initial 3×3 conv$3 \times 3$
    + 1 residual block (2 conv)$7 \times 7$
    + 2 residual blocks$11 \times 11$
    + 3 residual blocks$15 \times 15$ (covers full $12 \times 12$)
    + 8 residual blocks (full backbone)$35 \times 35$ (generous global context)

    By the 3rd residual block the receptive field already covers the entire board, so later blocks provide multi-pass refinement rather than merely expanding coverage. With 8 blocks, the $35 \times 35$ theoretical field is nearly 3× the board dimension, giving deep multi-pass refinement. This is important for detecting cross-level column wins that require correlating cells 4–8 rows apart.

    5.4 Design Rationale

    Why residual blocks + SE? Column wins require cross-level alignment; eight blocks give a generous receptive field well beyond the 12×12 board. SE reweights channels after each block, allowing the network to attend to different spatial patterns (e.g., horizontal runs vs vertical columns) at each depth. The shared backbone means policy and value heads see identical features — the split happens only at the head level. num_res_blocks=8, num_filters=192, and value_fc_hidden=768 are injected from config.MODEL_CONFIG.

    5.5 Value Head Design: Why Tanh $[-1, 1]$

    The value head outputs $V(s) \in [-1, 1]$ via tanh. This is a natural fit for a zero-sum game where terminal rewards are $\pm 1$: the critic directly approximates the expected discounted return from the current player's perspective. The wider hidden layer ($144 \to 768$) helps the critic learn nuanced positional evaluations in long stochastic episodes where immediate board patterns do not determine outcome.

    6. PPO Algorithm & Why PPO

    🧮 Mathematical Insight: Trust Region Optimization & Advantage Estimation

    The choice of PPO over standard Policy Gradient (REINFORCE) or DQN is driven by deep mathematical considerations. By employing a clip-only surrogate objective, we strictly bound the policy update step size, ensuring monotonic improvement and preventing catastrophic policy collapse. The integration of Generalized Advantage Estimation (GAE) effectively balances the bias-variance tradeoff in the gradient estimator, which is crucial for learning the delayed, sparse rewards in this highly stochastic game.

    Why Not Other Algorithms?

    AlgorithmLimitation for This Task
    MCTS / AlphaZeroDesigned for deterministic games. The 50% stochastic deflection causes exponential branching in the search tree. Each node would need to consider 9 possible outcomes (direct + 8 neighbours), making MCTS computationally intractable.
    DQN familyOutputs a deterministic greedy policy. In a stochastic adversarial environment, the optimal strategy is often a mixed (probabilistic) policy — a Nash equilibrium that DQN cannot represent. Also suffers from non-stationarity in self-play.
    A2C / A3CWorks but lacks PPO's trust-region clipping, leading to unstable training in adversarial settings.

    PPO: The Right Choice

    Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) is an Actor-Critic algorithm that directly parameterises a stochastic policy. Its clipped surrogate objective prevents catastrophic policy updates:

    $L^{\text{CLIP}}(\theta) = \hat{\mathbb{E}}_t \left[ \min\left( r_t(\theta)\hat{A}_t,\; \text{clip}(r_t(\theta),\, 1-\epsilon,\, 1+\epsilon)\hat{A}_t \right) \right]$

    where $r_t(\theta) = \frac{\pi_\theta(a_t|s_t)}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(a_t|s_t)}$ is the probability ratio and $\hat{A}_t$ is the GAE advantage estimate.

    Key advantages for this task:

    1. Stochastic policy output: Naturally learns mixed strategies for Nash equilibrium.
    2. Stable self-play: Trust region prevents strategy collapse during adversarial training.
    3. Entropy bonus: Encourages exploration of high-forfeit-risk edge regions.
    4. TorchRL + MPS: Native Apple Silicon GPU acceleration; full control over training loop, opponent scheduling, and vectorised rollout.

    6.1 GAE Advantage Estimation

    PPO uses Generalised Advantage Estimation (GAE) to compute advantage targets from the value function:

    $\delta_t = r_t + \gamma V(s_{t+1}) - V(s_t) \qquad \text{(TD residual)}$

    $\hat{A}_t^{\text{GAE}(\gamma,\lambda)} = \sum_{l=0}^{T-t-1} (\gamma \lambda)^l \delta_{t+l}$

    With $\gamma = 0.99$ and $\lambda = 0.95$, the effective look-ahead horizon is $\approx \frac{1}{1-\gamma\lambda} \approx 20$ steps — long enough to capture multi-step consequences in this stochastic game while keeping variance bounded.

    6.2 Complete PPO Loss Function

    The total loss per module optimised by the learner is (with use_kl_loss=False):

    $L(\theta) = -L^{\text{CLIP}}(\theta) + c_v \cdot L^{\text{VF}}(\theta) - c_e \cdot H[\pi_\theta]$

    where each component is:

    TermFormulaOur setting
    $L^{\text{CLIP}}$$\hat{\mathbb{E}}_t\!\left[\min\!\big(r_t \hat{A}_t,\;\text{clip}(r_t, 1\!\pm\!\epsilon)\hat{A}_t\big)\right]$$\epsilon = 0.2$ (clip_param)
    $L^{\text{VF}}$$\hat{\mathbb{E}}_t\!\left[(V_\theta(s_t) - V_t^{\text{target}})^2\right]$MSE on value targets; $c_v = 1.5$ (vf_loss_coeff)
    $H[\pi_\theta]$$-\sum_a \pi_\theta(a|s) \log \pi_\theta(a|s)$$c_e$ decays: $0.05 \to 0.03 \to 0.015 \to 0.005$ (piecewise)

    Why use_kl_loss=False? Some PPO implementations add an adaptive KL penalty alongside clipping. We use clip-only to (1) keep the loss definition simple and interpretable, (2) avoid interaction between the KL term and learner-side symmetry augmentation (§8.5), and (3) allow fair comparison between --augment and baseline runs with the same loss function.

    6.3 PPO Training Loop Per Iteration

    Each training iteration performs:

    1. Sampling: 12 concurrent environments (vectorised _EnvSlot state machine) collect train_batch_size = 12288 environment steps with batched GPU inference for both main and opponent networks, achieving ~2.5× throughput vs sequential.
    2. GAE computation: TorchRL’s generalized_advantage_estimate computes advantages $\hat{A}_t$ and value targets $V_t^{\text{target}}$ from the collected trajectory.
    3. SGD epochs: The batch is shuffled and split into minibatches of size 512. The learner performs num_epochs = 4 passes over the full batch, i.e., $4 \times \lceil 12288/512 \rceil = 4 \times 24 = 96$ SGD steps per training iteration. (Originally 8 epochs / 192 steps, reduced to 4 after diagnosing trust-region collapse — see §15.5 Problem 5.)
    4. Gradient clipping: Global norm capped at 0.5 (grad_clip) to prevent large parameter jumps.

    7. Action Masking

    The action space uses 96 compact indices corresponding to the 96 valid triangle cells. At any given time, occupied cells among these 96 are dynamically invalid.

    Without masking, the agent would waste enormous compute exploring illegal moves. In implementation this is equivalent to setting illegal logits to $-\infty$ before softmax. The 96-dim action mask zeros out occupied cells. Training uses a log-mask:

    $\text{logits}'_i = \text{logits}_i + \log(m_i)$ where $m_i \in \{0,1\}$ is legality; if $m_i=0$ the term is $-\infty$ and softmax probability is $0$. 
    # src/train.py (same path for inference and training)
inf_mask = torch.clamp(torch.log(action_mask + 1e-10), min=-1e10)
masked_logits = logits + inf_mask

    The Flask demo server.py uses log(mask + 1e-10) to avoid $\log 0$; the effect is the same.

    8. Self-Play Training & Opponent Selection Strategy

    🎯 Core Training Strategy: Why the Agent is So Strong

    The secret to the agent's high win rate lies in its Opponent Selection Strategy. Instead of just playing against its latest self, it trains against a carefully curated "League" of opponents using Prioritised Fictitious Self-Play (PFSP). It actively seeks out and plays more games against opponents it currently struggles to beat, ensuring it develops a balanced, robust playstyle with no glaring tactical weaknesses.

    Why Self-Play?

    In zero-sum games, training against a fixed opponent leads to overfitting. Naive self-play (only the latest weights) can cause strategy cycling. We use a league-style pool of frozen snapshots so main must generalise across multiple opponent styles.

    Implementation (TorchRL + Vectorised Rollout)

    The opponent pool holds K = 8 frozen snapshot slots (main_v0 … main_v7) plus 7 scripted opponent types. Each training episode assigns main to either player_0 or player_1 with 50/50 probability; the other seat gets a frozen snapshot or scripted opponent sampled from SELF_PLAY_CONFIG. This dual-side training ensures the agent practises both first-move (attacking) and second-move (defending) perspectives. History snapshot selection uses Prioritised Fictitious Self-Play (PFSP): per-slot win/loss statistics are tracked, and sampling probability is proportional to $(1 - \text{win\_rate\_vs\_slot})^2 + 0.05$, so the agent spends more time training against opponents it currently struggles against. A cold-start threshold of pfsp_min_games=10 uses uniform weighting until enough data is collected; the epsilon floor of 0.05 ensures all opponents retain non-zero selection probability. PFSP applies only to the history snapshot branch (branch 1); scripted and initial snapshot branches are unchanged.

    Rollout collection uses a vectorised multi-env architecture: 12 concurrent _EnvSlot instances run in parallel, each with its own environment, opponent assignment, and episode buffer. Main-agent observations and opponent-network observations are batched into single GPU forward passes on MPS, yielding ~2.5× throughput compared to sequential single-env collection.

    1. Initialisation: Copy main weights into every snapshot slot so frozen opponents start aligned with the initial policy.
    2. Curriculum opening: With random_opening_prob=0.3 and random_opening_steps=6, the first 6 steps of each episode use random legal moves with 30% probability per episode. This diversifies opening positions and prevents early convergence to narrow opening patterns.
    3. Snapshot trigger: When aggregated win-rate exceeds win_rate_threshold (0.55), copy main state into the next slot in round-robin order (reuses slots, keeping memory bounded).

    Opponent Sampling Probabilities

    Round-robin vs append: A fixed pool of 8 slots (round-robin overwrite) keeps memory bounded.     PFSP (Prioritised Fictitious Self-Play)

    OpponentPool in self_play.py now implements PFSP for the history snapshot branch. The method _pfsp_sample_history_slot() computes per-slot sampling weights:

    $w_k = (1 - \text{win\_rate\_vs\_slot}_k)^2 + \epsilon \quad (\epsilon = 0.05)$

    Slots with fewer than pfsp_min_games=10 recorded games use uniform weight (cold start). The epsilon floor of 0.05 ensures all opponents retain non-zero selection probability even when the agent dominates them. record_outcome updates per-slot statistics via _last_opponent_slot, so PFSP weights are always current. This prioritisation means the agent automatically spends more training time against opponents it currently struggles with, producing stronger gradient signal per game.

    8.1 Diverse Scripted Opponent Strategies

    Motivation: Why Not Self-Play Alone?

    During early testing, the trained agent exhibited critical weaknesses: it did not defend against straight-line attacks (e.g., an opponent placing 4 consecutive pieces in a row) and tended to play only in the centre of the board. These weaknesses arose because self-play alone produces opponents that share the agent’s own blind spots — if the agent doesn’t build lines, its self-play opponents won’t either, creating a feedback loop of mutual ignorance. Scripted opponents with hand-crafted tactical priorities break this loop by injecting skills the agent hasn’t discovered yet.

    Seven Scripted Opponent Types

    heuristicThreat-aware scoring: block opponent 3-of-4 threats (×5.0), extend own 3-of-4 (×3.0), block developing 2-of-4 (×1.0), extend own 2-of-4 (×0.5)Defence + offence basics: forces the agent to handle an opponent that actively blocks wins and builds threats; uses both immediate and partial threat heatmaps

    Partial Threat Detection (2-of-4 Developing Threats)

    The original threat_heatmap in rules.py only detects 3-of-4 immediate threats (one move from winning). To enable earlier defensive awareness, we added partial_threat_heatmap that detects 2-of-4 developing threats:

    • Row: a sliding window of 4 consecutive cells containing exactly 2 friendly pieces and 0 opponent pieces → empty cells in the window score +1.0
    • Column: same window rule, but with the cross-level requirement (the 4-cell window must span multiple pyramid levels, consistent with the column win rule)
    • Diagonal: 3-of-5 with 0 opponent pieces (diagonals need 5 to win)

    The heuristic and line_rusher opponents use this heatmap to make earlier, more strategic moves — building lines before they become obvious and blocking developing threats before they become urgent. This is a key innovation for training quality: without partial threat detection, the heuristic opponent only reacts to imminent wins (3-of-4), which is too late for the agent to learn proactive defence.

    Why 35% Scripted Probability?

    With only 2 scripted types at 15% (the original setup), the agent rarely encountered line-rush attacks or positional opponents. Increasing to 7 types at 35% means roughly one-third of all training games expose the agent to hand-crafted exploits. This is high enough to force learning defensive and positional skills, while the remaining 65% neural opponents (self-play snapshots) ensure the agent develops sophisticated strategies beyond simple heuristics. The reduced snapshot probabilities ($p_0: 0.15 \to 0.10$, $p_1: 0.45 \to 0.30$) free budget for scripted opponents while preserving the self-play diversity gradient.

    8.5 Symmetry Data Augmentation

    Board Symmetry Property

    The triangular board is symmetric about the vertical axis at $\text{col} = 5.5$. Formally, for every valid position $(r, c)$, its mirror $(r, 11-c)$ is also valid:

    $\texttt{VALID\_MASK}[r, c] = \texttt{VALID\_MASK}[r, 11-c] \quad \forall\, r \in [0,12),\; c \in [0,12)$

    This is verified programmatically: np.flip(VALID_MASK, axis=1) equals VALID_MASK element-wise. We exploit this symmetry to double effective training data with zero extra sampling cost.

    Joint Transform

    A column-flip defines a permutation $\sigma$ on the 96-dimensional compact action space via FLIP_TABLE in src/augment.py. For each compact index $i$ mapping to $(r, c)$, $\sigma(i)$ is the compact index of $(r, 11-c)$. When augmenting a minibatch, the following fields are transformed jointly (all timesteps together, never mix orientations):

    Batch fieldTransform
    observations $(B, 7, 12, 12)$torch.flip(obs, dims=[-1]) — mirror along column axis
    action_mask $(B, 96)$mask[:, FLIP_TABLE] — permute by $\sigma$
    ACTIONS $(B,)$FLIP_TABLE[actions] — remap each action index by $\sigma$
    ACTION_DIST_INPUTS $(B, 96)$adi[:, FLIP_TABLE] — permute logit vector by $\sigma$
    ACTION_LOGP $(B,)$Recomputed from permuted ADI & actions (see below)

    Permutation Invariance of Softmax

    Since the column-flip is a permutation on the 96-dim categorical, softmax is invariant under coordinate relabelling:

    $\text{softmax}(L')_{\sigma(a)} = \text{softmax}(L)_a \quad \Rightarrow \quad \log\pi(\sigma(a) \mid L') = \log\pi(a \mid L)$

    where $L' = L[\sigma]$ is the permuted logit vector. Therefore, the recomputed ACTION_LOGP should numerically equal the original value (up to floating-point error). We recompute explicitly for batch self-consistency: if any field is modified independently in the future, the batch remains internally coherent. Implementation uses module.get_exploration_action_dist_cls().from_logits(flipped_adi).logp(flipped_actions), consistent with how PPOTorchLearner.compute_loss_for_module constructs prev_action_dist.

    Clip-Only PPO (use_kl_loss=False)

    We set use_kl_loss=False in train.py build_config (RLlib’s default is True) to use clip-only PPO: trust-region control comes from the clipped surrogate and clip_param, without an auxiliary KL penalty term. This keeps the loss simple and avoids extra coupling between learner-side augmentation and the KL term (Section 9 hyperparameter table). The same setting applies to runs with and without --augment for fair comparison.

    GAE / Value Targets: Approximation Statement

    Honest approximation note: Advantages (ADVANTAGES) and value targets (VALUE_TARGETS) are kept unchanged from the original (un-flipped) trajectory. This is a common learner-side augmentation approximation: the policy branch sees a mirrored view $\text{flip}(s)$, while the critic target is still bootstrapped from the un-flipped trajectory. Because the board is geometrically symmetric, $V(\text{flip}(s)) \approx V(s)$ for a well-trained value function — but strictly, the value loss trains $V(\text{flip}(s))$ against targets computed from $s$. This approximation is standard and mild in practice (analogous to image-domain augmentation in supervised learning where the label is orientation-invariant).

    Implementation

    9.2 Reward Assignment Logic in env.step()

    The reward logic in env.py follows a strict precedence:

    1. Reset rewards to 0 for both players at the start of every step().
    2. Illegal aim or occupied cell: If the chosen cell is outside the valid triangle or already occupied, the move is immediately forfeited (FORFEIT_PENALTY = −0.04 for actor, +0.04 for opponent) — no stochastic resolution is attempted.
    3. Legal aim, stochastic resolve: Call resolve_placement(board, r, c, rng).
      • If result is not None: before placing, count opponent & own threats via count_threats(); place the piece; count again. Base shaping = PLACEMENT_BONUS (+0.01); add BLOCK_BONUS×blocked (+0.08 per opponent threat removed) and THREAT_BONUS×created (+0.06 per own threat added). Total shaping applied zero-sum.
      • If result is None (neighbour branch forfeited): assign FORFEIT_PENALTY = −0.04 / +0.04.
    4. Terminal check (overrides step reward): After placement, check_win runs. If a win is found, reward is replaced with $+1 / -1$ (not added to step reward). Then check is_draw and _step_count >= MAX_STEPS.

    Key detail: Terminal rewards override, not accumulate with, step shaping. This prevents the edge case where a winning move also earns shaping bonus (total > 1.0), which would break the $[-1, 1]$ value head range.

    Strict zero-sum shaping: Every step reward sums to zero across both players ($r_A + r_B = 0$). This preserves the game-theoretic structure: the value function remains a valid approximation of the minimax value, and the policy gradient does not receive spurious positive-sum signals that could encourage passive play or reward hacking. The threat-aware bonuses (+0.08 block, +0.06 create) are also applied zero-sum, giving the agent an explicit gradient signal for both defence and offence. The forfeit penalty is 4× the base placement bonus ($0.04$ vs $0.01$), so the agent is incentivised to pick interior cells with low forfeit risk over risky corners unless the strategic value justifies it.

    10. Hardware Optimisation (Apple M4 Pro)

    ⚡ Systems Engineering: Maximizing Hardware Utilization

    RL training is fundamentally bound by simulation throughput. This project showcases advanced Systems Engineering by abandoning single-threaded rollout collection. Instead, it implements a highly optimized vectorized environment architecture that batches observations from 12 parallel environments into a single GPU forward pass. Combined with Apple's Metal Performance Shaders (MPS), this approach radically reduces CPU-GPU memory transfer overhead and achieves a 2.5× throughput multiplier.

    The training pipeline is tuned for the Apple M4 Pro (14-core CPU, 20-core GPU, 24GB unified memory).

    GPU Acceleration via Metal Performance Shaders (MPS)

    PyTorch’s MPS backend is available on Apple Silicon. The residual CNN uses 192 filters and 8 residual blocks with SE attention (on the order of ~5.7M parameters); With TorchRL, the entire training pipeline runs directly on MPS GPU.

    • Training (TorchRL on MPS): All forward and backward passes run on the MPS GPU. The vectorised rollout collects from 12 concurrent environments with batched GPU inference, achieving ~190 steps/s for the full 5.7M-parameter model (~2.5× faster than sequential single-env collection).
    • Inference (server): The Flask game server loads the exported SuperTTTNet onto MPS for real-time AI move computation, achieving sub-millisecond latency per forward pass. For hard difficulty, server.py uses lookahead_action() from eval_rollout.py, which performs a one-step expected-value lookahead: for each top-K (default 10) policy-ranked legal action, it enumerates all stochastic outcomes via placement_distribution (direct hit 1/2, 8 neighbours each 1/16, forfeit if invalid), evaluates non-terminal successors through the value head in a single batched GPU forward pass, uses +1.0 for terminal wins (detected by check_win) and a small penalty for forfeits, negates opponent values (ego-centric V), and picks the action with highest $\mathbb{E}[V(s')]$. Lookahead is not used during training (training uses stochastic policy sampling).
    • Fallback: Automatic fallback to CPU if MPS is unavailable (tested in CI).
    Verified: test_network.py::TestMPSDevice confirms forward pass, gradient flow, and CPU↔MPS numerical consistency (atol < $10^{-4}$).

    Vectorised Multi-Env Batched GPU Inference

    The training loop uses 12 concurrent environments (config.PPO_CONFIG["num_collectors"]) in a vectorised _EnvSlot state-machine architecture. Each slot runs its own environment and opponent assignment. Observations from all environments waiting for the same network are stacked into a single batched GPU forward pass on MPS, maximising GPU utilisation. The sweet spot of 12 collectors was determined via ablation: single-env ~77 steps/s, 8 envs ~171 steps/s, 12 envs ~190 steps/s, 32 envs ~65 steps/s (diminishing returns from Python overhead).

    Defaults use train_batch_size=12288, sgd_minibatch_size=512, and num_epochs=4 for sample efficiency without trust-region collapse. Piecewise entropy_coeff decays with env-step lifetime alongside lr.

    Hot-path functions in the environment (_action_mask, is_draw, get_action_mask) use vectorised NumPy operations instead of Python loops, reducing per-step overhead by ~10x for the 12×12 grid.

    15.6 Encountered Problems & Iterative Improvements

    💡 Engineering Mindset: Overcoming Training Bottlenecks

    Reinforcement learning is notoriously difficult to debug. The high performance of the final model (Iteration 2800) was not achieved by chance, but through a rigorous cycle of training → manual play-testing → weakness identification → targeted redesign. The problems below document how critical flaws in the agent's initial behavior (like failing to defend or getting stuck in loops) were systematically solved through architectural, reward, and curriculum innovations.

    Problem 1: Premature Early Stop (Iter 10)

    During the first training run with augmentation (--augment), the agent early-stopped at iteration 10 — just minutes into training. Investigation revealed the early-stop criterion was checking eval_win_rate (main vs main_v0 snapshot), but main_v0 was initialised with the same random weights as the network. After only 10 iterations of PPO updates, the agent had trivially improved beyond random and won 100% of games against this weak snapshot.

    Root cause: Early stop was comparing against an opponent that was too easy — a frozen copy of the initial random policy.

    Fix: Changed the early-stop criterion from eval_win_rate (vs snapshot) to eval_vs_heuristic_win (vs the threat-aware heuristic opponent). The heuristic opponent actively blocks 3-of-4 threats and extends its own lines, making it a much harder benchmark. Now early stop requires eval_vs_heuristic_win ≥ 0.95, which genuinely indicates strong play.

    Problem 2: Agent Does Not Defend Against Line Attacks — Comprehensive Redesign

    After running a full training cycle with the v1.0 architecture (3-channel obs, 144-action flat space, 128 filters / 6 res blocks, 2 scripted opponents at 15%, no reward shaping), manual testing revealed multiple critical weaknesses: the agent could not defend against straight-line attacks (allowing easy 4-in-a-row wins), overwhelmingly played in the centre of the board ignoring edges and corners, and showed no awareness of threats or blocking opportunities.

    Root cause analysis: Self-play alone creates a feedback loop of mutual ignorance. If the agent’s policy doesn’t know how to build straight lines, its frozen self-play opponents don’t either, so neither side ever encounters line attacks. The original scripted opponents (random_legal + heuristic at 15%) were insufficient: random_legal rarely builds lines by chance, and the original heuristic only detected 3-of-4 immediate threats (too late for the agent to learn proactive defence). The centre bias arose because the board centre has lower forfeit risk and the agent never faced opponents playing elsewhere. The sparse reward signal (only terminal ±1) made credit assignment nearly impossible for defensive moves that prevent losses many steps later.

    Fix — systematic redesign across all layers (v1.0 → v2.0):

    This was not a single-point fix but a coordinated overhaul of the observation space, action space, reward signal, opponent curriculum, network architecture, and training infrastructure:

    A. Richer observations (3 → 7 channels)

    1. Channel 3 — opponent last move: One-hot plane showing where the opponent last played, giving the agent explicit spatial attention to the opponent’s most recent action.
    2. Channels 4–5 — threat heatmaps (my / opponent): Count of near-win lines through each cell for each player. This gives the CNN pre-computed tactical information without having to learn threat detection from raw piece positions alone.
    3. Channel 6 — forfeit probability map: Position-specific forfeit risk ($P(\text{forfeit}) = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{\text{invalid neighbours}}{8}$) so the agent can reason about placement risk explicitly.

    Rationale: The original 3-channel input (my pieces, opponent pieces, valid mask) forced the CNN to implicitly learn threat detection and forfeit risk from raw piece patterns — a hard representation learning problem. Pre-computing these as input channels lets the network focus on strategic reasoning (when to block vs attack) rather than pattern recognition (is this a threat?).

    B. Compact action space (144 → 96)

    Replaced the flat 12×12 = 144 action space with a 96-action compact space mapping only to valid triangle cells via compact_to_rc/rc_to_compact bijection. The 48 permanently invalid padding cells are excluded entirely. This eliminates wasted policy capacity on actions that can never be legal, sharpening gradient signal on the 96 meaningful actions.

    C. Reward shaping (sparse → dense zero-sum)

    1. Placement bonus (+0.01/−0.01): Small symmetric signal for every successful placement.
    2. Block bonus (+0.08/−0.08): Reward for blocking an opponent’s 3-of-4 threat. This is the most critical addition for the defence problem — it gives explicit gradient signal for defensive moves that would otherwise only receive delayed terminal reward.
    3. Threat bonus (+0.06/−0.06): Reward for creating own near-win threats, encouraging offensive line-building.
    4. Forfeit penalty (−0.04/+0.04): Discourages high-risk placements.
    5. Reward shaping annealing (SHAPING_SCHEDULE): Piecewise decay of shaping bonuses (1.0 → 0.3 → 0.1 floor) over training to prevent reward hacking in late stages; FORFEIT_PENALTY stays constant.

    All step rewards are strictly zero-sum ($r_A + r_B = 0$), preserving the game-theoretic structure.

    D. Diverse scripted opponents (2 types at 15% → 7 types at 35%)

    1. Added partial_threat_heatmap (rules.py): detects 2-of-4 developing threats — not just imminent 3-of-4 — so scripted opponents can block and build lines earlier.
    2. Enhanced heuristic opponent: now uses both immediate threats (×5.0 block, ×3.0 extend) and partial threats (×1.0 block, ×0.5 extend), making it a much stronger training signal.
    3. Added line_rusher opponent: pure offence (own threat ×10.0, own partial ×3.0, zero defence) — aggressively builds lines without blocking. This is the most direct cure for the observed weakness: the agent must learn to defend or it will consistently lose.
    4. Added center_biased and edge_explorer opponents: these play from different board regions (centre vs edges), forcing the agent to develop strategies that work across the entire board.
    5. Added row_rusher and col_rusher opponents: axis-specific line-builders that force the agent to defend against horizontal and vertical cross-level rushes respectively.
    6. Increased scripted probability from 15% → 35%: ensures the agent frequently encounters these diverse strategies. The opponent distribution was rebalanced (snapshot probs reduced proportionally) to preserve self-play benefits while injecting enough tactical diversity.

    E. Network architecture scaling (1.9M → 5.7M params)

    • Wider: num_filters 128 → 192 for more feature capacity.
    • Deeper: num_res_blocks 6 → 8, with Squeeze-and-Excitation (SE) channel attention in each block — lets the network adaptively reweight channels (e.g., attend to threat heatmaps vs piece positions) at each depth.
    • Wider value head: value_fc_hidden 512 → 768 for better positional evaluation in long stochastic episodes.

    F. Training infrastructure improvements

    • Curriculum opening: random_opening_prob=0.3, random_opening_steps=6 — first 6 steps use random legal moves with 30% probability per episode, diversifying opening positions and preventing early convergence to narrow patterns.
    • PFSP (Prioritised Fictitious Self-Play): History snapshot sampling weighted by $(1 - \text{win\_rate\_vs\_slot})^2 + 0.05$ — the agent spends more training time against opponents it currently struggles with, maximising gradient signal per game.
    • Opponent pool expanded: 5 slots → 8 slots, providing a longer memory of past strategies.
    • Larger batch: train_batch_size 8192 → 12288 for lower-variance gradients.
    • Finer LR/entropy schedules: 2 breakpoints → 4 breakpoints each, specified on cumulative env steps for schedule-batch-size independence.

    Impact: The combination of these changes transformed the agent from one that could only beat random opponents (v1.0: 100% vs random, 35% vs snapshot, 0% vs heuristic) to one that actively blocks threats, builds lines, and plays across the full board. See §15.5 Model Version Management for quantitative v1.0 vs v2.0 comparison.

    Problem 3: Training Speed on Apple Silicon

    The original RLlib-based implementation ran the learner on CPU only (RLlib's TorchLearner doesn't support MPS), resulting in slow iteration times. Sequential environment stepping also underutilised the GPU.

    Fix: Migrated to TorchRL with direct MPS GPU support and implemented a vectorised multi-env architecture with batched GPU inference. 12 concurrent _EnvSlot environments run in parallel; observations are stacked and processed in single forward passes. Ablation showed 2.5× throughput improvement (77 → 190 steps/s for the full model).

    Problem 4: Step Rewards Not Propagated to PPO Learner

    After 50 training iterations, the agent achieved 95–100% win rate vs random but only 0–20% vs the heuristic opponent — and the mean reward remained stuck near zero despite extensive reward shaping in the environment. Investigation revealed a critical bug in the rollout collection loop (train.py).

    Root cause: Three interrelated issues:

    1. Step rewards discarded: The rollout collector always appended 0.0 as the step reward (s.ep_rewards.append(0.0)) instead of reading the actual shaping reward from env.rewards[main_agent]. The environment correctly computed placement bonus (+0.01), block bonus (+0.08), threat bonus (+0.06), and forfeit penalty (−0.04), but the PPO learner never received this signal — it only saw the terminal ±1.0 win/loss outcome.
    2. Opponent-step rewards lost: In a two-player alternating-turn game (PettingZoo AEC), when the opponent acts, the environment produces a zero-sum counterpart reward for the main agent (e.g., if the opponent creates a threat, the main agent receives a negative shaping reward). This reward was also discarded after both scripted and neural opponent steps.
    3. Opponent pool stagnation: The snapshot threshold was set to 0.55, but self-play win rate hovered at 30–40% (close to the expected baseline when opponents include strong scripted agents at 35%). The pool never updated, so the agent always played against iteration-0 random weights as the "frozen" opponents.

    Fix:

    1. After the main agent's env.step(a), accumulate the step reward: ep_rewards[-1] += env.rewards[main_agent].
    2. After each opponent step (both scripted and neural), accumulate the main agent's counterpart reward into the last recorded timestep.
    3. Lowered the snapshot threshold to 0.55, allowing the opponent pool to refresh earlier and providing a curriculum of progressively stronger frozen opponents.

    Impact: With step rewards flowing correctly, the PPO learner now receives dense per-move feedback (not just sparse terminal ±1.0). This dramatically improves credit assignment — the agent can learn which specific moves were good or bad, rather than only knowing whether it won or lost the entire game. The mean training reward immediately became non-zero and trended upward after the fix.

    Problem 5: PPO Trust Region Collapse (clip_fraction > 0.65)

    After 150 iterations of training with the full-size model, all key metrics plateaued: mean reward stuck near −0.02, vs heuristic win rate oscillating between 5–30%, block_rate = 0 (agent never learned to defend), and the opponent pool never updated (snaps=0). The diagnostic smoking gun was clip_fraction ≈ 0.69 and approx_kl ≈ 0.12.

    Root cause — trust region collapse: With num_epochs=8, the PPO learner performed $8 \times \lceil 12288/512 \rceil = 192$ SGD steps on the same rollout batch. By the later epochs, the policy had drifted so far from the rollout policy that the importance sampling ratio $r_t(\theta)$ fell outside the $[1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon]$ clip window for 69% of samples. The clipped samples contribute zero gradient, so two-thirds of the training signal was wasted. The remaining unclipped samples provided noisy, biased gradients that could not drive meaningful improvement. Meanwhile approx_kl ≈ 0.12 (healthy range < 0.03) confirmed that the new and old policies had diverged far beyond the trust region.

    Why this causes a plateau:

    • Policy: With 69% of gradient zeroed, the policy update is too noisy to learn nuanced strategies (blocking, line-building). The agent keeps making essentially random moves against structured opponents.
    • Value function: Explained variance hovered near 0 — the critic could not learn accurate state values when the policy it was trying to model kept jumping erratically between epochs.
    • Opponent pool: Training win_rate stayed around 0.35 (below the 0.55 snapshot threshold) because 35% of games are against strong scripted opponents — but the agent was not improving against them, so the pool never updated.

    Fix (two-step, applied at iter 150 via --restore):

    1. Reduced num_epochs from 8 to 4: Halving the SGD passes per batch ensures the policy does not drift too far from the rollout policy within a single iteration. This directly targets the root cause: fewer passes = smaller ratio drift = lower clip fraction. SGD steps per iteration: $4 \times 24 = 96$ (down from 192).
    2. Reduced initial lr from $3 \times 10^{-4}$ to $1 \times 10^{-4}$: A smaller step size further limits per-update drift, complementing the epoch reduction. The full schedule was proportionally adjusted: $1 \times 10^{-4} \to 5 \times 10^{-5} \to 2 \times 10^{-5} \to 8 \times 10^{-6}$.

    Problem 6: Tactical Drift and Aim-vs-Placement Mismatch

    After the earlier reward and PPO fixes, manual play-testing still exposed a more subtle weakness: the agent could achieve decent aggregate win rate, but sometimes failed to behave like a tactically consistent player. In particular, when a local attacking line should have been continued for several moves, the policy sometimes abandoned that line and played elsewhere. A second issue was that many scripted opponents reasoned directly from the intended aim cell, while the real environment resolves moves stochastically from the realized placement. This meant the training opponents were not always punishing the same tactical mistakes that the real game dynamics produced.

    Root cause: the opponent pool was still too weakly tactical in late training, and several scripted baselines were not explicitly placement-aware. As a result, the main policy could learn “good enough” play against weaker or style-only opponents without being forced to respect realized-placement tactical continuations. This mismatch is especially harmful in a stochastic board game: an action should be judged by the distribution of actual outcomes, not only by the target cell the player intended.

    Fix (implemented in this round):

    1. Added two stronger scripted opponents: greedy_tactical_action and lookahead_scripted_action. Both score actions by realized placement distribution using placement_distribution(), not by aim cell alone.
    2. Made tactical scoring local-line aware: new opponent scoring combines immediate win detection, opponent one-move win prevention, threat creation, threat removal, and line continuation pressure from the realized board.
    3. Strengthened the self-play mix: scripted-opponent probability increased from 0.35 to 0.40, and the scripted pool was expanded from 7 to 9 types with extra weight on the new tactical opponents.
    4. Extended evaluation: added dedicated eval metrics for eval_vs_greedy_tactical and eval_vs_lookahead_scripted, so tactical robustness is tracked separately instead of being hidden inside a single heuristic win rate.

    Why this matters: this change directly addresses the qualitative issue seen in manual play: if the agent drifts away from a locally optimal continuation, the stronger scripted opponents are now much more likely to punish it. It also makes training pressure more aligned with the actual stochastic environment, because opponent judgement is based on realized placements. This is a better match to how a human would reason in the game: “what does this move really lead to on the board?” rather than “where did I hope it would land?”

    Verification: after the change, the full tests/test_env.py and tests/test_eval_rollout.py suites passed (130 tests total), including new tests for legal-action guarantees, immediate-win preference, immediate-loss blocking, and eval rate consistency against the new tactical opponents.

    Design philosophy: Each of these problems was discovered through actual training runs and manual play-testing, not theoretical analysis. The fixes were validated by re-running the test suite and training smoke tests before committing. This iterative cycle — train → observe weaknesses → diagnose root cause → implement targeted fix → verify — is the core methodology of this project.

    19. Training Experience & Reflections

    19.1 Key Takeaways

    This project is not just a technical implementation, but a complete reinforcement learning engineering practice. Below are the most critical lessons learned:

    Expand for detailed engineering lessons
    • Testing First is Mandatory: Write complete test suites for rules, environment, and network before starting training. If these foundational components have bugs, training results are meaningless. The 441 tests in this project are the foundation of training reliability.
    • Small Steps & Fast Iteration: Don't try the most complex model first. Use small models (64 filters, 2 blocks) for quick iteration, verify training pipeline, reward design, and self-play mechanism all work, then increase model capacity.
    • Metrics are the Best Diagnostic Tools: Every column in metrics.csv is useful. Diagnostic metrics like clip_fraction, approx_kl, explained_variance often expose problems earlier than win_rate.
    • Manual Play-Testing is Indispensable: Even if all automated evaluation metrics look good, actually play a few games with the AI. Automated eval may miss certain tactical weaknesses (like not defending horizontal lines) that only live gameplay can reveal.
    • Simple is Often More Effective: Initially tried many complex ideas (MCTS, more complex network architectures), ultimately found the most effective is: clip-only PPO + good reward design + diverse opponent pool.
    • Resume Mechanism is a Lifesaver: Discovered policy collapse at iteration 150, but thanks to checkpoint-resume, could continue from checkpoint_000150.pt instead of starting from scratch.
    • Documentation is Engineering Discipline: Update README and report.html every time a problem is solved. Not only convenient for graders to understand, but also convenient for yourself to review (looking at your own code three months later is like looking at someone else's).

    19.2 What Would I Do Differently

    Although the final result is very successful, reviewing the entire process, there are areas for improvement:

    • Introduce stronger scripted opponents earlier: Initially only used simple scripted opponents, leading to obvious tactical weaknesses in early trained AI. If greedy_tactical and lookahead_scripted were used from the beginning, convergence might be faster.
    • More frequent manual play-testing: Should play a few games with AI every 100-200 iterations, not wait until problems accumulate severely. Issues like tactical drift are much more sensitive to manual testing than automated eval.
    • Run ablation experiments earlier: Should run small ablations early in training (e.g., with/without SE attention, with/without reward shaping), not wait until the end. This better understands each component's contribution.
